Visualization 2 - Regional map

An analysis of the geographic concentration of first names. The farther a curve falls below the diagonal, the more geographically concentrated the name is.
Source: INSEE first-name records, 1900–2020, aggregated by department (Corsica coded as "20"; overseas departments 971–974 included). The population reference includes all births recorded in each department over the period, including rare names. For each name, departments are accumulated in ascending order of overrepresentation (share of the name ÷ share of births). The resulting Lorenz curve remains below the diagonal, and the area between the curve and the diagonal determines the Gini concentration index displayed for each selection: approximately 0 indicates a geographically uniform distribution, while values approaching 1 indicate strong geographic concentration. Because the reference covers the full period, demographic change over time may also influence the measured concentration.
Overview — 1,184 first names: popularity and geographic concentration (select a point to display its curve)

The Gini index measures how unevenly a name is spread across departments. It equals twice the area between the curve and the diagonal: 0 means the name is distributed exactly like the overall population (perfectly uniform), while values approaching 1 mean it is concentrated in very few departments. The lower a curve dips below the diagonal, the higher its Gini index.
